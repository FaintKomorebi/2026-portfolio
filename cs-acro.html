Fig. 1 — ACRO Automation Systems · Homepage redesign

ACRO's 29-page website was burying a 90-year manufacturing reputation. Site visitors were already finding the solutions section — it was the most-visited content on the site, despite sitting three clicks deep. I restructured the information architecture around where intent actually peaked: solutions on the homepage, at scroll one, organized by industry. 29 pages became 17. The content stayed. The friction didn't.

Overview
01 — Context

90 years of expertise, buried in the wrong structure.

ACRO had built something real over 90 years: a reputation for precision manufacturing that larger competitors couldn't match on provenance alone. What they hadn't built was a way to communicate it. A procurement lead evaluating ACRO against three competitors had maybe 90 seconds to decide if they were worth a call. The site gave them no quick answer.

The brief was a UI refresh within new brand guidelines. I pushed back on that framing early. The visual problems were symptoms — the structure was what was actually failing customers. A redesign that cleaned up the aesthetics without fixing the navigation would have looked better and performed the same. The build needed to live in WordPress, managed by ACRO's SEO team post-launch, which shaped every architectural decision I made.

The brief Modernize the site UI around new brand guidelines, reduce complexity, and build a platform that scales with ACRO's future.
The real problem I identified the structure as the core issue early. 29 pages of nested content wasn't a maintenance problem. It was actively destroying conversion by forcing customers to navigate to answers the site already had.
Success looked like A site where a first-time visitor could confirm ACRO serves their industry, understand what ACRO builds, and find a path to contact. No wasted clicks.

Solutions visible on the homepage — zero clicks required

I eliminated every page that existed to route customers rather than serve them. The intermediate landing pages were organizational artifacts: they reflected ACRO's internal structure, not how a buyer thinks. Collapsing them into direct destinations cut the path to solutions from three clicks to one.

Decision
02 — The Key Decision
"Analytics showed me where customers were going. I stopped making them work to get there."

Google Analytics showed ACRO's solutions section was the most visited part of the existing site. Customers were actively seeking it out despite the friction. The conventional approach would have kept it buried inside a navigation menu. I pulled it directly onto the homepage as the first scroll after the hero, fully clickable and organized by industry.

It was a deliberate structural break from how the old site was organized, and leadership pushed back on it initially. I brought the analytics into a meeting and walked through exactly where customers were going and how many steps stood between them and that content. The data resolved it. If I knew where intent peaked, designing to meet it there wasn't a creative choice. It was the only defensible one.

Throughout the process I ran internal review sessions with the team: engineers and stakeholders who represent exactly the kind of buyer evaluating ACRO. The question we kept asking was simple: if you landed here trying to figure out whether ACRO can build what you need, does this get you there? Every structural decision got walked through that lens before it was finalized.

Considered Keep solutions on a dedicated interior page Standard structure, but added a click and a page load between interest and information.
Considered Lead with a full-page hero video like competitors Would have matched the landscape, not differentiated from it. Memorable doesn't follow the crowd.
Chosen Surface solutions on scroll-one of the homepage Analytics confirmed it was the most sought-after content. Reducing that friction became the central structural decision of the redesign.
Final Solution
① Getting There Quickly All of ACRO's solutions are visible in the first scroll on the landing page.
② Ease of Use By forgoing the Solutions landing page and giving users a dropdown list in the navbar, they're able to see all solutions and directly visit that page in one click.
① Industry-led entry point I organized solutions by industry rather than product category because that's how a buyer frames their problem. Not "I need automated assembly" but "I'm in automotive, does ACRO work in my space?" The industry labels answer that question before the customer has to ask it.
② Consolidation over elimination Pages that were artificially split across multiple destinations got merged into single, scrollable layouts. The content didn't shrink. The distance to it did. Scrolling through a page is faster than deciding which link to click next.
③ Closing the loop This case study opens with ACRO's credibility being buried. I designed this screen to be the specific moment that changes: the first view where a customer sees industry relevance, product capability, and a contact path simultaneously. No scroll required.

What I'd do differently

I'd push harder on the content strategy earlier. The structural decisions were sound, but several solution pages launched with copy that was holdover from the old site, accurate but written for search bots rather than real humans. We solved the architecture problem. The content problem is ongoing, and I should have scoped it explicitly rather than treating it as out of scope.
