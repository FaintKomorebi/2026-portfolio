Overview
01 — Context

A 40-person company with no shared understanding of where anything lives.

ACRO ran on 10+ mapped network drives — G, I, J, K, L, Q, T, X, Z, and others — each evolved independently, without structure or ownership. Departments had their own drives but also saved to shared ones. Personal folders lived next to company-wide resources with no separation. Nobody had a clear mental model of where to look for anything. Nobody was wrong to be confused.

A Microsoft 365 migration was already planned. The question was whether to drag the existing mess into SharePoint, or use the migration as a forcing function to design something intentional. The company needed a file system that worked for the people using it every day — not just one that was technically migrated.

The brief Migrate department files off legacy network drives into SharePoint. Move fast where possible.
The real problem No information architecture existed to migrate into. A direct copy would relocate the chaos, not fix it.
Constraints Engineering CAD deferred — requires a PDM system, not SharePoint. Marketing media excluded — SharePoint's media UX is too poor. Varying tech literacy across ~40 staff. One software tool (RoboGuide) silently fails on paths longer than ~100 characters.

One rule: company-wide = Resources, dept-specific = Team Site.

The rule eliminates ambiguity at the system level. Users don't guess which drive to check — they know whether they need a company form (Resources) or a department document (Team Site), and that answer is always the same.

Discovery
02 — Research

Survey first. Structure second.

Rather than arriving with a structure, discovery was designed to surface what already existed — how people actually worked, where files actually lived, and what the pain points actually were. A 14-person survey went across Engineering, Sales, Admin, Machining, and Shop. Stakeholder meetings with department leads followed. Every mapped drive was inventoried: file types, ownership, and SharePoint readiness.

Two discoveries changed the project scope. J: drive had organically evolved into a de facto company-wide forms library — users already had a mental model for it. The Resources page was built directly on top of it rather than replacing it. K: drive revealed the opposite: an anti-pattern where personal folders for nearly every employee sat alongside shared department files with no separation or governance. That doesn't get migrated. The migration is the forcing function to end it.

Decision
03 — The Key Decision
"A migration is a design opportunity. The mess wasn't random — it was the company's latent information architecture waiting to be made intentional."

The biggest failure mode in an enterprise file system is ambiguity: people don't know where to look, files get duplicated, and no version is ever clearly the right one. The two-tier architecture was built on one rule — company-wide files live in Resources; department-specific files live in Team Sites. That rule makes every file decision obvious. There's no grey area, no "which version is current."

Departments were not asked to let someone organize their files for them. They were given the destination structure and the responsibility to clean their own drives before migration. This is change management as design: if a third party organizes it, nobody owns it. Adoption fails when people feel a system was done to them rather than with them.

The pilot department was HR — 2 users, all Word and Excel, low risk, already in motion. Every lesson learned (structure, permissions, migration tool behavior, user friction) gets baked into the playbook before rolling to larger departments. A pilot should be low-stakes and high-signal.

Considered Single flat library — all files in one place Simpler to set up. Without departmental separation, shared-drive problems recur in a new location.
Considered Organize the files for each department Faster short-term, but departments don't own what they didn't build. Adoption depends on ownership. Ruled out on change management grounds.
Chosen Two-tier: Resources + Department Team Sites One clear rule eliminates ambiguity. Departments own their own structure. The architecture scales to new departments without redesign.
Decisions
D-01 HR as the pilot department

2 users. All Word and Excel. Low risk, high signal. Every friction point discovered in HR gets resolved before Sales or Engineering sees it. Lessons compound into a reusable playbook.

Risk Management · Iterative Rollout
D-02 Flat folder structure to start

Every department begins with a single flat layer of top-level folders. No subfolders until the department proves they need them. Deep hierarchies are how drives become unnavigable.

Information Architecture · Scalability
D-03 Old drive stays read-only during transition

After migration, the source drive remains accessible read-only for 2–3 weeks. Change anxiety is real. A safety net removes fear, which removes resistance. Access removed only after department sign-off.

Change Management · User Psychology
D-04 SPMT over drag-and-drop

Microsoft's SharePoint Migration Tool maps source paths to destinations, produces a migration log, and is repeatable and auditable. Drag-and-drop at volume loses files, drops metadata, and leaves no record of what moved where.

Technical Product Thinking · Data Integrity
D-05 Engineering CAD gets a different solution

SharePoint is a document management platform. SolidWorks files have parametric references, assembly dependencies, and version control requirements SharePoint cannot handle. Engineers asked for a PDM system by name. Scoping what NOT to do is as important as scoping what to do.

Systems Thinking · Platform Selection · Scope Definition
D-06 Designing for Copilot before it exists

M365 Copilot search quality is directly tied to how well files are named and organized. Structure decisions are being made with Copilot in mind now — so the company gets compounding value from it on day one, with no second cleanup project required.
